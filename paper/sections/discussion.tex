\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the schedule buys, and what it does not.} Across MLPs and a
small transformer, the picture is consistent: cheap front steps carry most
of the per-step progress; deep credit assignment is needed occasionally, in
\emph{consecutive} doses, at moments a loss plateau identifies. At
${\sim}4$--$6\%$ full-depth budgets, \fw{} recovers roughly two-thirds of the
front-to-full accuracy gap on MLPs and outperforms or matches every fixed
schedule we tested (Sections~\ref{sec:exp2}, \ref{sec:exp3})---but it does
not match always-full training
($1.3$--$3.3$ points on MLPs; a larger gap in validation loss on the
transformer, where it recovers about a fifth of the front-to-full gap). \fw{}
is a compute-quality trade, not a free lunch, and the trade steepens with
task difficulty in both directions: adaptivity matters more, and full depth
remains further out of reach.

\paragraph{What we believe transfers.} Four mechanisms, not the numbers.
(i)~\emph{Nested beats partitioned}: depth truncation is exclusive to the
top block, so compute-aware schedules should treat burst timing and block
choice as separate decisions---and our ablation shows the timing is the
active ingredient. (ii)~\emph{Burst-as-measurement with backoff}: any
scheduler that must decide whether expensive credit assignment is still
productive faces the cried-wolf problem; exponential backoff is a simple,
robust answer. (iii)~\emph{Isolated deep steps are dangerous}: interleaved
partial-training schemes need burst structure, and under Adam neither
gradient clipping nor regularization substitutes for it. (iv)~\emph{The
optimizer fires with the BP, gently}: moment estimates are part of the
credit-assignment machinery, should not outlive inter-burst gaps, and must
re-enter with a warmup---fresh state at full aggression is worse than stale
state; the episodic formulation also removes trunk optimizer memory for the
vast majority of steps.

\paragraph{What we do not claim.} All experiments are small (MLPs; a 10.8M
transformer on 1MB of text) with three seeds; absolute numbers are far from
competitive by design. We have not tested \fw{} at scale, under distribution
shift, with pretrained initializations, or against learned-schedule
baselines; the front was always the topmost block, and other cheap subspaces
(low-rank, sparse) are untested. The origins study
(Section~\ref{sec:exp1}) is scoped by its own depth result: local wiring
rules match dense training only in shallow networks.

\paragraph{Open directions.} Making the trigger's implicit estimate of
inequality~\eqref{eq:greedy} explicit (cheap stochastic probes of
$\|\nabla L\|$) would turn \fw{} into an approximate optimal stopping rule.
The episodic-optimizer ablation suggests a broader design space:
per-burst warmup lengths, moment half-lives matched to burst spacing, and
trust-ratio update clipping as the Adam-compatible safety net. At scale, the
memory argument sharpens---trunk optimizer state that exists only ${\sim}6\%$ of
the time is a different systems regime---and the natural next testbed is a
pretrained model, where \fw{} becomes an adaptive fine-tuning schedule and
\lpft{} is the one-switch special case it generalizes.

\section{Conclusion}
Most training steps do not need deep credit assignment; the hard part is
knowing which ones do. \fw{} answers with a closed-loop schedule: cheap
front steps by default, consecutive bursts of full backpropagation when---and
only when---a plateau says the cheap subspace is exhausted, and exponential
backoff when a burst reveals the plateau was global. At a few percent of the
full-backprop budget it beats or matches every fixed schedule we tested on
MLPs and beats all of them on a transformer at low budget. Two exceptions are
stated where they occur: a well-timed freezing ratchet edges \fw{} on MNIST
(within seed noise) and at mid-budget transformer compute, though its
required timing is catastrophic when transferred across testbeds. Where
fixed schedules fail, the failures localize to mechanisms we could measure
(isolated-step churn, stale optimizer moments) and repair (bursts, episodic
state). Credit assignment is a budgeted resource; \fw{} is a first controller for
spending it, and its backoff cap is the dial that chooses the budget.
