\section{Front wheeler on a transformer}
\label{sec:exp3}

We now change both the architecture and the optimizer: from SGD-trained
MLPs to a transformer under AdamW.

\paragraph{Protocol.} Character-level language modeling on tiny
Shakespeare: a 10.8M-parameter decoder-only transformer (6 layers,
$d{=}384$, 6 heads, context 256, dropout $0.2$), AdamW ($3{\times}10^{-4}$,
200-step warmup, cosine decay, weight decay $0.1$, clip $1.0$), 5{,}000
steps, batch 64, 3 seeds. The front is the last transformer block, the final
LayerNorm, and the LM head; the LM head is \emph{untied} from the token
embedding so front steps cannot leak trunk updates through weight tying.
Front steps run the trunk under \texttt{no\_grad}. Schedules as in
Section~\ref{sec:exp2}; metric is validation loss.

\paragraph{Only adaptive bursts convert a small budget into progress.}
Table~\ref{tab:exp3matrix} is the canonical comparison; read it within
matched backward-compute tiers. \textsc{full} reaches $1.488 \pm 0.008$;
\textsc{front} $1.836 \pm 0.004$ (Figure~\ref{fig:exp3pareto}). Between those anchors the fixed schedules
barely move: \lpft{} at a $10\%$ budget gains $0.003$ over front-only
($1.832 \pm 0.004$), and periodic interleaving is \emph{destructive} at
every budget tested---$1.87$ at $2\%$, worsening monotonically with
interleaving frequency to $2.40 \pm 0.05$ at a $20\%$ budget, far worse
than doing nothing deep. \fw{} at a ${\sim}6\%$ budget reaches
$1.773 \pm 0.013$, statistically indistinguishable from \lpft{} spending
$50\%$ ($1.766 \pm 0.007$)---an $8\times$ ratio in deep-step budget and ${\sim}2.7\times$ in backward
compute over the one-switch schedule---robust across thresholds ($1.773$--$1.776$ for
$\tau$ varied $10\times$), with \bcd{} tracking at $1.797 \pm 0.010$.
The gap to always-full remains substantial: \fw{} recovers ${\sim}18\%$
of the front-to-full gap here, vs.\ ${\sim}$two-thirds on MLPs. Deep
trunks need more deep training on harder tasks; \fw{} trades quality for
compute rather than matching full BP.

\paragraph{The backoff is a budget dial; the threshold is a floor.}
Sweeping the trigger threshold $\tau$ across two orders of magnitude
($0.005 \to 1.0$) with the governor active barely moves the operating
point: the backoff cancels eager alarms and the budget stays pinned at
$6$--$9\%$ of steps. The lever that works is the governor itself: sweeping
the cooldown cap $\kappa_{\max}$ from $2000$ down to $50$ traces a smooth,
monotone Pareto curve---budgets $6\% \to 49\%$ of steps, validation loss
$1.773 \to 1.596$ (Figure~\ref{fig:exp3pareto})---that dominates \lpft{} and
periodic at every tested budget on the transformer: at an $8\%$ budget the
dial already matches \lpft{} spending $50\%$, and its $49\%$ endpoint
($1.596 \pm 0.009$) closes more than two-thirds of the front-to-full gap
at half the deep-step budget. On the CIFAR-10 MLP the dial's high-budget
endpoint (a sweep not reported in Section~\ref{sec:exp2}) is comparable
to, but does not beat, the best fixed $50\%$ schedules ($53.8$ vs.\
$54.2$ for \lpft{}); the dominance claim is
specific to the transformer. One parameter thus selects the
compute--quality operating point, with no retuning of the trigger.

\paragraph{The ratchet is fragile where \fw{} is not.} At \fw{}'s exact
backward-FLOP budget, \textsc{ratchet}($0.1$) collapses to $2.271 \pm 0.04$---worse
than periodic---because it freezes five of six blocks before the trunk has
learned anything, and a ratchet cannot un-freeze. \textsc{ratchet}($0.3$) does beat
\fw{}'s default ($1.696$ vs.\ $1.773$) at $1.46\times$ the compute, and
remains slightly ahead of the \fw{} dial at matched mid-budget FLOPs
($1.696$ at $555$k~GF vs.\ $1.717$ at $584$k~GF): a \emph{well-timed}
\textsc{ratchet} is genuinely competitive in the mid-budget regime. Together with the MLP results
(Section~\ref{sec:exp2}), the pattern is consistent: a well-timed ratchet
is competitive, but its timing is task-critical and unrecoverable when
wrong, whereas \fw{} ran identical trigger hyperparameters on every
testbed in this paper. Revocable bursts buy robustness that irrevocable
freezing cannot.


\paragraph{Interleaving toxicity is optimizer-dependent and clipping does
not save Adam.} The periodic failure is the MLP collapse of
Section~\ref{sec:exp2} in a new guise, with two differences. First, damage
scales monotonically with interleaving \emph{frequency} (worse at period 5
than 50), implicating repeated perturbation---each isolated deep step moves
trunk features under a head sharply co-adapted to the old ones, and the
system churns instead of converging---rather than any single bad step.
Second, it happens \emph{despite} gradient clipping, which cured the SGD
case: Adam's update $m/\sqrt{v}$ is approximately scale-invariant in the
gradient, so clipping the gradient does not clip the step. Trunk moment
estimates also go stale across gaps ($\beta_2 = 0.999$ means a 50-step burst
refreshes only ${\sim}5\%$ of $v$), the schedule-induced analogue of the
sparse-update pathology that motivates SparseAdam for embedding tables.

\paragraph{Episodic optimizer state: the optimizer fires with the BP.}
If moment estimates are part of the credit-assignment machinery, their
\emph{state} should be episodic like the bursts themselves. We test four
variants: \textsc{persist} (status quo), \textsc{reset} (trunk moments
cleared at burst entry), \textsc{reset-warmup} (reset plus a 10-step
in-burst learning-rate warmup, mirroring fine-tuning practice when
unfreezing), and \textsc{episodic} (Adam state exists only inside bursts;
front phases run on SGD). Table~\ref{tab:optstate} gives the verdict, which
refined our hypothesis. \textsc{reset-warmup} \emph{rescues} periodic
interleaving almost entirely ($2.05 \to 1.82$, from far worse than
front-only to slightly better): the dominant pathology of isolated deep
steps under Adam is stale-state re-entry at full aggression, not
perturbation per se. But \textsc{reset} alone \emph{backfires}
($2.05 \to 2.33$): a freshly initialized Adam step is the most aggressive
kind, so hygiene without gentleness is worse than staleness. The principle
that survives is sharper than the one we started with: \emph{the optimizer
must fire with the BP, and it must fire gently}---bursts amortize a warmup
naturally, while length-1 episodes can only approximate one by running deep
steps at reduced learning rate (for periodic, our 10-step warmup on
length-1 episodes reduces to exactly that, a caveat for interpretation).
Even fully rescued, periodic at a $10\%$ budget ($1.82$) does not reach
\fw{} at $6\%$ ($1.77$): adaptive placement retains its advantage. For
\fw{} itself the variants are a wash ($1.773$ persist vs.\ $1.773$
reset-warmup)---bursts already re-equilibrate internally---which yields the
systems result for free: \emph{trunk optimizer state can be discarded
between bursts at no quality cost}, so Adam's two fp32 moments per trunk
parameter need exist only during the ${\sim}6\%$ of steps spent in bursts.
The strict \textsc{episodic} variant fails here ($2.13$), attributable to
its SGD front phases (transformers train poorly without adaptivity); a
stateless-but-adaptive front optimizer is the natural repair and is future
work.

\paragraph{Measured resources validate the cost model---with one caveat.}
End-to-end single runs (2{,}000 steps, identical hardware;
Appendix~\ref{app:infra}) measure: full BP
$98.4$s, \fw{} $49.3$s, \textsc{ratchet}($0.1$) $49.7$s, front-only $46.6$s---\fw{}
and the flops-matched ratchet are equal-cost in wall clock to within $1\%$,
and \fw{} delivers a real $2.0\times$ end-to-end speedup over full BP at
this scale (per-step: $23.3$ms front vs.\ $49.2$ms full). The caveat is
peak activation memory: front-only peaks at $0.77$\,GB, but any schedule
that ever takes a full-depth step---\fw{} included---peaks at $3.03$\,GB,
because the peak is set by the deepest single step. \fw{} reduces
\emph{average} memory and (via episodic state) trunk optimizer memory, not
the activation peak; activation checkpointing inside bursts, which are only
${\sim}6\%$ of steps, is the natural remedy and is future work.

\subsection{Is the trigger necessary? Controls and a distribution shift}
\label{sec:controls}

Two simpler rivals could explain \fw{}'s results without any closed loop:
\textsc{ftlp} (spend all depth first, then never again) and \textsc{pburst}
(bursts on a fixed clock). We test both on the stationary task and under a
mid-training distribution shift.

\paragraph{Stationary: deep-first is the highest-peak, lowest-valley
schedule.} At \fw{}'s matched budget, \textsc{ftlp}($0.06$) is the worst
cell in our entire study ($2.290 \pm 0.035$, far below \textsc{front}):
$300$ full steps from initialization---before warmup completes---leave a
half-trained trunk that is then frozen forever. Yet given $3\times$ the
budget, \textsc{ftlp}($0.2$) is the \emph{best} cheap schedule we measured
($1.665 \pm 0.007$ at a $20\%$ deep-step budget), ahead of the \fw{} dial
($1.717$) and \textsc{ratchet}($0.3$) ($1.696$). Deep-first is a bet on
knowing the right early budget: its peak and its collapse are two settings
of the same parameter. \textsc{pburst} at matched budget is consistently
behind \fw{} ($1.814 \pm 0.014$ vs.\ $1.773 \pm 0.013$) though far from
failure; note its period was set \emph{from \fw{}'s own discovered
budget}---the clock is an oracle baseline.

switch at step $5{,}400$, placed mid-gap for \textsc{pburst}'s clock so it
cannot be lucky; schedules are not told). Post-shift validation loss,

Three observations (Table~\ref{tab:shift}). First, \fw{} is the best
cheap schedule post-shift, ahead of the fair-placed clock in all three seed
pairings ($+0.002$, $+0.028$, $+0.005$)---a small, consistent edge---and
its result is invariant to where the shift lands, which no clock can claim.
Its burst log shows the mechanism: the trigger stayed silent while front
steps productively re-fit the head to the new corpus (${\sim}1{,}000$
steps), then fired ahead of its own backoff trajectory once that cheap
adaptation saturated. Second, \textsc{ftlp} falls from best cheap schedule
(stationary, right budget) to the \textsc{front} floor: all its depth was
spent on a corpus that no longer exists. Third, the shift is mild (English
prose to English prose) and compresses the cheap field into $0.07$ of loss;
the dramatic failures in this study occur on \emph{stationary} tasks with
wrongly-set schedule parameters, not under shift.

\paragraph{The trigger's verdict, stated at full strength and no more.}
Across every test: consecutive bursts are the load-bearing mechanism; a
fixed clock with an oracle period ties or slightly trails \fw{}; the
trigger's demonstrated contributions are (i) discovering the burst schedule
without the oracle ($\tau$ inert across $200\times$, identical
configuration on every testbed, budget self-selected per task), (ii) a
small consistent edge stationary ($+0.04$) and under shift ($+0.01$), and
(iii) placement invariance. One honest weakness: post-shift, \fw{}
recovered $12\%$ of the front-to-full gap versus $18\%$
stationary---the backoff that protects it on stationary tasks had
lengthened its cooldown pre-shift, capping how fast it re-escalates;
a shift-aware governor reset is an obvious next design.

The results so far take the premise---that cheap front steps carry most
per-step progress---as given; the experiments that supplied it are reported
in Appendix~\ref{sec:exp1}.
