\section{Front wheeler on MLPs}
\label{sec:exp2}

\paragraph{Protocol.} MLPs with four hidden layers of width 512; SGD momentum
$0.9$, learning rate $0.05$, global gradient-norm clipping at $1.0$; 20
epochs (MNIST) / 30 epochs (CIFAR-10); 3 seeds. \fw{} uses $C{=}50$,
$B{=}50$, $\kappa_{\max}{=}2000$, $\tau \in \{0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05\}$
(MNIST) / $\{0.005, 0.02, 0.05\}$ (CIFAR-10).
Baselines: \textsc{full} (BP every step), \textsc{front} (never full),
\textsc{periodic}-$N$ (full every $N$th step, $N \in \{2,5,10,20,50\}$),
\lpft{} (front until a switch point, then full; switch swept over
$\{0.5, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95, 0.96\}$ of training), \bcd{} (identical trigger
and backoff, but bursts update the trunk with the head frozen---the
complementary-block variant), and \textsc{ratchet}($r$), which is compared
on the backward-compute axis and therefore reported separately from
Table~\ref{tab:exp2}.

\paragraph{Adaptive beats fixed and one-switch at matched budget.}
Table~\ref{tab:exp2} and Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} compare schedules at the
operating point \fw{} selects on its own (${\sim}4\%$ full steps). At its
best threshold, \fw{} beats the matched periodic schedule by $+1.5$ (MNIST)
and $+5.1$ (CIFAR-10) points, and beats one-switch \lpft{} by $+2.0$ and
$+6.1$; the comparison does not hinge on threshold selection, since the
\emph{worst} $\tau$ still wins by $+0.9$ and $+3.6$ against the strongest
matched fixed baseline. \fw{} at $4\%$ budget also beats periodic and
\lpft{} at $10\%$ budget on CIFAR-10 ($+1.8$ and $+2.0$), and edges them on
MNIST ($+0.5$ and $+0.4$, comparable to the seed spread). The margins
\emph{grow} with task difficulty: the harder the problem, the more it
matters \emph{when} full credit assignment is spent, not merely how much.
\fw{} shows no tuning cliff ($\tau$ varied $10\times$ moves accuracy by at
most $0.5$ / $0.9$ points on MNIST / CIFAR-10), and concedes $1.3$ (MNIST)
and $3.3$ (CIFAR-10) points to always-full BP. At
generous budgets the picture reverses in an instructive way: with $50\%$ full
steps, \lpft{} is the best schedule on both datasets ($98.0\%$ / $54.2\%$),
consistent with its design regime \citep{kumar2022finetuning}. Adaptivity
pays precisely in the scarce-budget regime.

\paragraph{The freezing ratchet: strong when timed right, and timing is
task-critical.} The progressive-freezing baseline (\textsc{ratchet};
Section~\ref{sec:framework}) comes in two settings---\textsc{ratchet}($0.1$)
(all-but-front frozen by $10\%$ of training) and \textsc{ratchet}($0.3$)
(by $30\%$)---and is compared in backward compute, since its per-step cost varies
as layers freeze. At matched compute, \textsc{ratchet}($0.1$) exceeds \fw{} on MNIST
consistently but not significantly ($97.3$ vs.\ $97.1$; positive in all
three seed pairings, $+0.22$ on average, within one seed-std; \textsc{ratchet}($0.3$) reaches $98.2$ at a
third of full BP's FLOPs) and trails \fw{} on
CIFAR-10 ($50.8$ vs.\ $51.6$ at matched FLOPs). We therefore do not claim
\fw{} beats all fixed schedules on easy tasks; the decisive contrast
appears on the transformer (Section~\ref{sec:exp3}), where the same
\textsc{ratchet}($0.1$) setting is catastrophic---frozen-forever layers cannot recover from
premature freezing, while \fw{}'s bursts are revocable by construction.

\paragraph{The trigger, not the block, carries the benefit.} \bcd{} shares
\fw{}'s closed-loop trigger but spends bursts on the complementary block
(trunk only). It ties \fw{} on MNIST and trails by $1.1$ points on CIFAR-10.
The scheduling mechanism therefore transfers across block structures nearly
intact; joint bursts add a modest gain only on the harder task. Note,
however, that \bcd{} bursts cannot be made cheap (Section~\ref{sec:framework}):
only the nested scheme converts its cheap phase into compute savings.

\paragraph{Isolated full steps are dangerous.} Without gradient clipping,
periodic interleaving collapsed to chance within the first epoch on all three
seeds at $N \in \{20, 50\}$, and on one of three seeds at $N{=}10$: the front-trained, increasingly confident head
back-propagates error into a trunk it has drifted away from, with trunk
gradient norms growing to ${\sim}20\times$ their initial scale before the
collapse, driving hidden units dead. \fw{}'s \emph{consecutive} bursts partly
mitigate this (the trunk re-equilibrates within a burst), and global
norm-clipping removes it entirely; all reported runs clip at $1.0$. This
is a from-scratch analogue of the feature-distortion argument for \lpft{}
\citep{kumar2022finetuning}, here observable as a training-stability
failure rather than an out-of-distribution effect. Clipping closes the
issue under SGD; Section~\ref{sec:exp3} shows that under Adam it does not,
which is where the schedule's interaction with the optimizer becomes the
story.

\paragraph{Compute accounting.} We report budgets in backward
multiply--accumulates (Section~\ref{sec:framework}) because end-to-end wall
clock at this scale is dominated by the harness (data loading, kernel launch,
per-epoch evaluation): all schedules complete in ${\sim}16$s (MNIST) /
${\sim}20$s (CIFAR-10) per run on an RTX PRO 6000. Isolated per-step timing
confirms the proxy translates once GEMMs dominate: a front step is
$1.4\times$ faster than a full step at width 512/depth 4 (batch 128), rising
to $2.5$--$2.7\times$ at widths 4096--8192 and depths 8--16 (batches
512--1024), approaching the ${\sim}3\times$ limit implied by a backward pass
costing ${\sim}2\times$ the forward; the measured ratios slightly exceed the
GEMM-only prediction because the trunk's no-grad forward also skips
activation bookkeeping. Since \fw{} takes ${\sim}96\%$ front steps, its
amortized step cost approaches the front-step cost at scale.
