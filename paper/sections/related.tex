\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Supermasks and the lottery ticket hypothesis.} \citet{frankle2019lottery}
showed that dense networks contain sparse subnetworks trainable in isolation;
\citet{zhou2019deconstructing} observed that a mask alone---applied to
\emph{untrained} weights---already performs well, and \citet{ramanujan2020whats}
made this practical with edge-popup, finding subnetworks of random networks
that match trained accuracy. \citet{malach2020proving} and
\citet{pensia2020optimal} proved that sufficiently overparameterized random
networks contain such subnetworks with high probability. \citet{gaier2019weight}
demonstrated architectures that perform without weight training. All of this
establishes that the \emph{target} of connectivity search exists; the search
itself, however, has been gradient-based. Our Axis~1 asks what happens when the
search rule is restricted to local information.

\paragraph{Local learning rules.} Feedback alignment \citep{lillicrap2016random}
replaces the transposed forward weights in the backward pass with fixed random
matrices; direct feedback alignment \citep{nokland2016direct} broadcasts the
output error directly to every hidden layer. Three-factor rules combine a
Hebbian eligibility trace with a global neuromodulatory scalar
\citep{fremaux2016neuromodulated}, the scalar case being REINFORCE-style
learning \citep{williams1992simple}. Forward-forward \citep{hinton2022forward}
avoids a backward pass altogether. We do not propose a new local rule; we
transplant existing rules from \emph{weight} updates to \emph{mask-score}
updates, so that the locality axis can be varied while the hypothesis class
(subnetworks of a fixed random network) is held constant.

\paragraph{Scheduling and partial credit assignment.} Decoupled training
replaces the global backward pass with learned local surrogates
\citep{jaderberg2017decoupled} or greedy layer-wise objectives
\citep{belilovsky2019greedy}; FreezeOut \citep{brock2017freezeout} anneals
early layers out of training on a fixed schedule. Linear probing followed by
fine-tuning (\lpft{}) \citep{kumar2022finetuning} is the one-switch special
case of our Axis~2, motivated in the transfer setting by feature distortion:
a randomly initialized head sends large, misaligned gradients into good
pretrained features. Block coordinate descent with greedy (Gauss--Southwell)
block selection \citep{nutini2015coordinate} is the classical framework for
adaptive block updates; its theory assumes uniform per-block costs. Our
setting differs in that the cheap block is the \emph{only} block that admits
depth truncation (Section~\ref{sec:framework}), making the cost asymmetry,
and hence the scheduling problem, the object of study. Straight-through
estimation of discrete masks follows \citet{bengio2013estimating}.
