\title{How Much Backpropagation Do You Need?\\
\large The Price of Local and Infrequent Credit Assignment in Two Controlled Settings}

\author{Mamdouh Alramadan\\
XTAM Research Labs\\
\texttt{research@xtam.ai}}

\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Backpropagation supplies every parameter with a globally informed,
per-step error signal. We ask how much of that signal is actually needed, by
removing it along two independent axes in controlled MLP settings.
\textbf{Axis 1 (locality):} we freeze all weights at random initialization and
train only a binary connectivity mask, comparing mask-search rules that use
progressively less global information: full gradients (edge-popup), a fixed
random projection of the output error (direct feedback alignment applied to
mask scores), and a single global scalar per sample (a three-factor Hebbian
rule). \textbf{Axis 2 (frequency):} we train normally but replace most
backpropagation steps with cheap updates of the last hidden layer and readout
only, triggering full-depth bursts by a plateau detector with exponential
backoff (\fw{}, ``front wheeler''). On MNIST and CIFAR-10 we find:
(i) gradient-based mask search over frozen random weights matches---and on
CIFAR-10 \emph{exceeds}---dense weight training ($55.9\%$ vs.\ $53.0\%$);
(ii) an error-vector broadcast is sufficient for local mask search
(within $1$--$3$ points of the gradient upper bound), while a global scalar is
not ($26$--$52\%$), locating the information threshold between a vector and a
scalar; (iii) plateau-triggered bursts using ${\sim}4\%$ full-backprop steps
beat periodic schedules and one-switch linear-probe-then-fine-tune at matched
budget, by margins that \emph{grow} with task difficulty ($+1.5$ to
$+6.1$ points); and (iv) an ablation shows the closed-loop trigger, not the
choice of parameter block, carries the benefit. We also document two failure
modes---a sign subtlety in straight-through mask gradients and a dead-ReLU
collapse caused by isolated full-backprop steps---that we believe are useful
to practitioners. All experiments are small-scale by design; we characterize,
rather than compete.
\end{abstract}
